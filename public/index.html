Long residence rules changed in April 2024 ILR after 10 years now requires 10 years of continuous lawful residence — time spent overstaying breaks the clock (Appendix Long Residence). Residence history, decisions and compliance

These questions apply to everyone and determine eligibility for long-term routes.

Have you ever been outside the UK for more than 180 days within a 12-month period?
Absence warning More than 180 days outside the UK in any rolling 12-month period breaks continuous residence for ILR on most routes, with limited exceptions (serious illness, conflict, some overseas postings). You can request your travel history from UKVI to check.
Have you ever had a visa or immigration application refused?
Are you currently challenging a Home Office decision? For example a refusal you are appealing, or a decision under administrative review
Upload your decision letters Deadlines are short (usually 14 days in the UK). Use the Your documents panel to upload your refusal letter or appeal papers so the dates and grounds are captured with your saved session.
Have you ever been deported, removed or subject to a re-entry ban from the UK?
Re-entry ban or deportation history A previous removal or deportation can bar re-entry for up to 10 years depending on the circumstances. Specialist legal advice is essential.
What is your level of English? Required at B1 level for most ILR and citizenship applications
Have you passed the Life in the UK Test? Required for ILR and citizenship unless you are under 18 or 65 and over

Section 6 of 6

Final details

A few last questions to complete your assessment profile.

Have you ever used false documents or deception in a UK application?
Deception has serious consequences Using false documents or deception can result in a 10-year ban and refusal on suitability grounds. Take specialist legal advice before any new application.
Are you currently receiving any public funds? For example Universal Credit, Housing Benefit or Child Benefit
No Recourse to Public Funds (NRPF) Most visa holders are subject to NRPF. Claiming public funds while subject to NRPF breaches your conditions and can affect current and future applications (GOV.UK guidance).
